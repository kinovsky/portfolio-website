Vela

About

Vela is a responsive web app dedicated to providing easy to understand and intuitive displays for real-time weather, wave, and wind conditions, as well as forecasts and reports. The application sets itself apart from its competition by including safety info, as well as recommendations for nearby amenities and attractions for users.

Problem

Users need simple access to a variety of weather-based information that will keep them informed and safe while at the beach or out on the water. Many weather apps can confuse and stress out new users, lacking easy-to-read displays or educational information they need to understand terms pertaining to the information they seek. Users also may not be familiar with safety precautions they need to take in order to stay safe during their activities or when inclement weather affects them.

Solution

An app that grabs from reliable weather API to forecast weather, wind, and waves, but also allows users to submit live updates when sudden shifts in conditions happen in their location. A user-friendly interface will aid users in better understanding complex weather terms and reports rather than confusing or intimidating them and instead, will put them at ease while engaging in their activities. Vela will provide safety information and step-by-step guides in the event of an emergency while on the beach or on the water.

Research

Competitive Analysis

In order to better understand the weather-based application space, I analyzed numerous weather-based apps for their key objectives, strategies, market advantage, UX/UI design, and SWOT profiles. These apps range from ones that strive to be general use for just about any user to utilize, or attempt to fill a niche for particular professions or hobbies, such as surfers, fishers, drone pilots, etc. I chose to focus on the top-rated apps on the iOS and Google Play store, along with apps mentioned by users that were interviewed or responded to a Google survey. Two key apps I chose to focus on were Windy.app and Windy.com.

Summary

Wind, waves and weather forecasts for surfers, kitesurfers, windsurfers, sailors, fishermen and other wind sports. Features include:

  • Local weather forecasts from NOAA for 10 days with 3 hours step.
  • Over 30,000 spots sorted and located by type and area.
  • Community involvement through spot chats and reporting weather changes as they happen.

Summary

A fast, intuitive, detailed, and accurate weather app aimed at aiding professional pilots, paragliders, skydivers, kiters, surfers, boaters, fishermen, storm chasers, and general weather geeks, as well as by governments army staff, and rescue teams.

  • 40+ different weather maps including wind, rain, temperature, humidity, pressure, and swell (waves).
  • Global satellite composite from NOAA, EUMETSAT, and Himawari with image frequency update of 5-15 minutes.
  • 1500+ paragliding spots and nearby webcams available on their map.

User Interviews & Survey

Over the course of a week, I interviewed 4 individuals to better understand their history with weather-based apps and how they come into play in their everyday lives. Once completed, I created an affinity map to better organize my findings for later reference. The goals of these interviews were to:

  • Discover how users plan their beach trips and activities around weather conditions.
  • Determine strengths and weaknesses of existing apps.
  • Identify user wants and goals when using these apps.
  • Understanding their biggest pain points and how they can be addressed.

While interviews were being conducted, I sent out a survey to various social media channels to gain more feedback from a variety of users and get a bigger picture of who I might be designing for and gain additional perspectives on user’s experiences with weather apps. Below are affinity maps of the responses I received from my user interviews, organized and color coded to refer back to easily.

Key Insights

  • Most users complain that the weather apps they commonly use can be unreliable and untrustworthy whether the information is incorrect, data is missing, or usability is frustrating.
  • Most users want information for nearby attractions, restaurants, shops, and parking close to beaches and water activity spots.
  • Safety is important, even to the most stubborn user, to fall back on when in doubt.
  • Weather, wind, crowds, air quality, and waves/tides were the most popular choices that might impact a user's decision to partake in their beach trips or activities.

User Personas & Flows

With competitive analysis and user interviews wrapped up, I developed user personas that represent Vela’s target audience in order to empathize with the app’s key users and better understand their problems and goals and how they can be addressed. I crafted 2 key personas (Thelma & Joe) based on the product requirements, as well as an outlier persona (Darrell) for edge cases.

To better align user needs with business goals, I fleshed out journey maps to ideate the navigation structure of the Vela application prior to creating a sitemap and wireframing. Each persona possesses unique goals and needs, so it was integral that the application properly address these aspects of our users.

Wireframing & Prototyping

Low-Fidelity Wireframes

With a refined sitemap, it came time to bring my design ideas to life. With the business goals, user needs, and navigational structure in mind, I used Adobe Photoshop, a Wacom Intuos tablet, and the brush tool to sketch out what the Vela app would roughly look like, creating the foundation of what would later become a high-fidelity, clickable prototype.

Mid-Fidelity Prototype

Transitioning to Adobe XD, I translated my low-fidelity sketches into mid-fidelity wireframes. Switching to this platform presented many opportunities to refine the design in real-time, discover numerous improvement opportunities off the bat, and reassessing the foundation of the design. Once these wireframes were assembled, it was time to add in UI elements and really give the application life for the first time.

High-Fidelity Prototype

After I felt comfortable with the structure of my mid-fidelity wireframes, it was time to create a clickable high-fidelity prototype that included all the application’s main features and allowed users to perform all the app’s key functions. This step included incorporating UI elements, including color, icons, copy, and animations. This is the version of the app that was used for usability testing.

Usability Testing

6 moderated, remote usability tests were run over the course of 4 days, with each person meeting with me over Zoom or Google Meet in order to record the interviews to later assess results as needed. Participants were given 5 tasks to complete during our meetings and were encouraged to think out loud when navigating through the app in order for me to gauge their feelings, emotions, and behaviors throughout the process. At the conclusion of testing, I created an affinity map and rainbow spreadsheet to analyze the results, and used Jakob Nielsen’s severity scale to determine what improvements were the most urgent to address moving forward. Below is the affinity map analyzing the results of each testing session, color coded by the respondees.

Preference Testing

After making a few changes to key wireframes, a preference test was conducted through Helio and sent directly to friends and family, along with multiple social channels; a total of 30 responses were recorded and analyzed. Users were asked to choose between variations of the app’s Splash Screen, Sign-Up, and Dashboard. The results from the usability and preference tests would influence how I approached the redesign of the application moving forward.

Refining the Design

At the conclusion of both usability and preference testing, I went back to the high-fidelity prototype to make further revisions, keeping in mind the pain points, frustrations, dead ends, and mistakes that users experienced while using Vela for the first time. This also gave me the opportunity to make changes based on elements of Material Design, emotional design, grids, and spacing. Before and afters of the splash screen and home page are shown below.

UI Style Guide

In order to convey the design to an engineering team and shareholders who are not working on the project, I created a UI Style Guide to showcase specific design aspects of the product, including colors, typography, iconography, navigation, imagery, and basic guidelines.

Design Language

With the UI Style Guide listing the set of standards expected for the overall product, I took to creating a Design Language System that dives more in depth and provides a guide for the overall aesthetics and style for a single platform.

Accessibility

In order to cater Vela to meet the needs of the visually impaired, accessibility features including screen reader, monochrome mode, and pinch to zoom were included to streamline their experience. Each feature can be accessed via the Settings tab, and once active, will display an icon at the top of the page to indicate it is in use.
